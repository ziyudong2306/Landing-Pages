How Sleep Is Connected To Back And Neck Pain

Through thermal imaging and advanced muscle monitoring equipment, he studied the sleep process of 1,000 people with pain.

Dr. Tanaka discovered that most sleep positions force your trapezius muscle—the crucial connector between your neck and shoulders—to stay tense all night. 

"Your muscles desperately try to keep your head and neck aligned, hour after hour, night after night," he explained.

This creates a devastating chain reaction:

First, your neck muscles lock up because they're forced to work overtime. These muscles tense up trying to hold your head in the right position. It's like asking them to do a plank exercise all night—they eventually seize up from exhaustion.

Then tension spreads to your shoulders because they're all connected. When your neck muscles lock up, nearby shoulder muscles tighten to help support the weight of your head. It's like a row of dominoes—once one muscle group gets strained, the next one takes on extra work until it also becomes overwhelmed.

Finally, the combination of locked neck muscles and tense shoulders pulls your spine out of its natural position. When your spine is misaligned, it compresses delicate nerves, causing pain to radiate throughout your body.

"Your bed isn't the problem," Dr. Tanaka said.

You can have the most expensive mattress in the world, but if your pillow doesn't support proper alignment, you're damaging your body every night.
